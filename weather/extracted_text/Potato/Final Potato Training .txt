1.0 BACKGROUND TO THE MANUAL (45 minutes)
1.1 ECONOMIC IMPORTANCE OF POTATOES
Potato (Solanum TuberosumL) has high productivity per unit area and its versatility in utilization
makes it the second most important food crop in Kenya after maize. Potatoes are grown twice
annually (long and short rains) and it is estimated 1.5 million tonnes are produced on about
161,000 hectares of land. This is worth between Ksh 40-50 billion each year and compares well
with annual maize production of 40 million bags worth Ksh 120 billion (Potato Strategy, 2016).
However, yields are generally low with an average yield of 10 tonnes per hectare (potential being
40 tonnes per ha achievable under recommended agronomic practices) due to a number of
challenges such as inadequate use of high quality seed potato and other inputs. Lack of proper
storage facilities on-farm and at the marketing centres has led to huge losses during marketing.
Farmers do not store potatoes due to lack of technical know-how; need for immediate cash and
poor quality produce due to pests, diseases or mechanical damage. The industry directly and
indirectly employs about 3.3 million actors, as producers, market agents, transporters, processors,
vendors, retailers and exporters.
1.2 JUSTIFICATION FOR THIS MANUAL
Potato farmer training is a key aspect in improving the livelihoods of small holder producers in
Kenya. Training is a learning process that involves acquiring knowledge, sharpening of skills,
concepts, change in attitude, behavior and functional capacity of an individual and institution
towards the achievement of a predetermined goal. However, many farmer trainers working for
various organizations conduct trainings using training materials or methodologies that are not
based on the principles of adult learners. Different trainers refer to different training materials
that give different recommendations, which end up confusing the farmers. Among the issues
that are not provided in a standardized way are planting methods, seed rates, timing of
agronomic activities like earthing up, de-haulming and post-harvest handling. Further there are a
number of agri-entreprenuers, among them the youth, who cannot access simplified and easy to
read materials on the various aspects along the potato value chain. This is despite the fact that
there are huge employment and business opportunities in investing and engaging them in the
potato value chain.
To address this issue a Training Needs Assessment (TNA) was carried out within a gender lens
among the farmers in Nyandarua County focusing on women and the youth involved in

1.2 FACILITATORS GUIDES ON MARKET ASSESSMENT
SESSION 1: MARKET ASSESSMENT(2 hours)
1. Climate Setting
A successful agri-preneur should begin with the market in mind. To identify the consumers of
the products (gender, age, lifestyles, and income levels), location of their customers, market
assessment will be important. This assessment will also help in understanding what form of the
products they want, when, at what price and any other relevant information.

--- Page 10 ---
Do a quick climate setting of the introduction to agri-preneurship and ask the trainees some of the following
questions: What is a market? Where do you sell your produce? What is the difference between a market and a
customer? Ask what they would consider when selecting a market and why. Ask the trainees if they carry a
market assessment before starting a new enterprise, growing a new crop or keeping new livestock. List the points
given on flip chart for reference later.
After this opening discussion, mention to them about the learning outcome for this learning activity
Learning Outcome
By the end of this learning activity the trainees should be able to conduct a market assessment and select
their customers in the market.
2. Why Conduct a Market Assessment?
Inform the trainees that in market assessment we seek to answer the following questions. In groups of two let them
answer these questions;
i. Who are my potential customers? – Their ages, location and characteristics
ii. What do they require? – The quality and quantity
iii. Does my product meet their needs or do i need to produce another product?
iv. How will they know I have what they need?
Let the groups present their answers. Conclude by facilitating a discussion on the benefits of market assessment
which include;
 Identify the customers and their needs.
 Determine how to reach the customers.
 Observe how prices and product volume changes over time.
 Assess the level of competition.
3. Information required in Market Assessment
Facilitate a guided discussion on the key areas while conducting a market assessment. Conclude the discussion by
making a presentation that there are things about the market that one will seek to understand when performing
market assessment. They include;
i. The number of customers in the market (Market Demand).
ii. How the customers will change over time (Market growth)?
iii. How much profits this markets can generate (Market profitability).
iv. How prices and volumes change over time (Market trends).
v. How to compete with others in the market (Competition and competitive advantage)

vii. Other products that can be used instead of my products (Substitutes)

4. Procedure for market assessment
Make a presentation on the key steps in conducting a market assessment. These are as follows;
Step 1 - Identify the specific customers by seeking answers to these questions;
 Are there existing customers not adequately served by the existing suppliers?
(Unsatisfied demand)
 Are there customers served by suppliers from elsewhere? (demand served by imports)
 Are there customers not served at all?
 Do you think the customers will increase in the near future?
If any of the answers to these questions is yes, then there are customers for your products.
Step 2 - Preparing for Field Work -In order to prepare well, the following should be done;
 Develop the aims for the visit
 Develop a checklist for collecting information
 Organize with the market outlets about the visit
 Plan with the participants on the materials required, roles, brief them about the
tools to use and how long it will take
 Organize the logistics for the visit- transport, lunch etc.
Step 3 - Field Data Collection using these tools;
 Observation methods – Observation schedules and recording

6. Can the Agri-Enterprise Make Money or Not?
Now that you have already assessed the markets and identified your customers, including what
they need and when the prices are best or when there is limited supply. You need to make an
assessment whether this agri-enterprise can make money or not. This will be based on the
findings from the market assessment.
Ask the trainees what they would look for when choosing an enterprise that can make money. List this points on
a flip chart. Facilitate a review of the points and conclude by making the summary that when assessing an
enterprise viability some of the things to consider include;
 Rewarding to the agripreneur (Cost benefit consideration)
 Potential for customers to increase in your enterprise.
 Specific target customers – aiming to satisfy customers with unmet needs
 Start-up capital required –The lower the start-up capital the easier to start.
 Initial fixed costs - Let the fixed costs be determined by growth phases
 Other suppliers to the customers
 Legal considerations on the enterprise

Farmers do not know relationship between land size, crops spacing and optimal amount of input
needed. They underestimate on inputs and hence get lower yield. They have inadequate skills in
inputs measurement and application using local containers.

in units like kilogram or litres. However, when a farmer is selling his produce in containers (kasuku/ bucket

tuber.
Ask them

5. Discussions
Usually if a farmer has adequate amounts to deliver to market, the income may cover marketing

market as a group. If a farmer is also engaged in marketing the produce and therefore does not sell
on the farm or at the farm gate, he/she incurs marketing costs. As such, his/her net income is
equal to the return at market price less that marketing cost.

SESSION 2: STEPS IN PROTECTING THE POTATO CROP FROM WEEDS
–WEEDING, HILLING AND ROUGING (3 hours)
Weeding and rouging
Weeds compete with seed potatoes crops for nutrients. They may also harbour pests and
diseases that affect potatoes. As soon as the crop emerges, weed control is important to avoid
weeds competing with the young potato plants. Undesirable plants in a seed potato field need to
be removed to ensure high quality standard of the harvested tubers. Undesirable plants include
plants infected by seed borne diseases (viruses or bacterial diseases) and off-types. Off-types are
plants in the seed crop field which don’t conform to the variety being grown. Volunteers are
plants grown from tubers left in the soil from a previous potato crop in that field. Volunteers are
often infected with diseases like those caused by viruses that can infect the new seed crop. They
may also be from another variety than the variety that is being grown. Growing season to ensure
purity of the desired variety as well as high health standard of the seed crop. In cases where there
are weeds which may serve as sources of diseases, they too have to be removed.
Equally important are hygienic measures to prevent spread of diseases. Tools and protective gear
used in seed crops should be disinfected and only used at the seed crop field in a particular field
and not in other crops.
Removal of undesirable plants during growth in the field is called rouging. During rouging
undesirable plants are removed together with any newly formed tubers, placed in a polythene
bag, taken away from the field and disposed safely either by burying in a deep pit or burning.
Rouging is done throughout to;
 Monitor weeds as they emerge
 Remove by rouging the weeds using hands
 Use hand hoes and jembes to remove weeds taking care not to injure the tubers for small
holder farms
 For large scale farmers use mechanised methods like tractors
 Where this is not possible seek advice on best herbicides
 Ridging can also be carried out as weeding is done
Caution –weeds are more vigorous during the wet season, tools may spread diseases in
the farm hence need for field hygiene

Ridging or Earthing Up
A potato plant produces tubers after crop
establishment and this process is triggered by cool
and dark conditions. As tubers enlarge, they require
complete dark conditions to avoid greening. High
loose soil volume is necessary to allow tuber
expansion without hindrance. Therefore, loose soil
Figure 24: Earthing Design
should be raised by earthing up as the plant grows.
Earthing up improves the tuber positions on the plant and improves the quality of tubers
produced besides protecting the crop from getting infested with Potato Tuber Moth (PTM).
Good earthing up ensures sufficient environment for root development and promotes uptake of
water and nutrients required for plant growth and tuber production. It also keeps the tubers cool
and minimizes tuber deformities.
Poor ridging leads to a high proportion of green tubers as well as to exposure of stolon tips
which instead of forming tubers give rise to secondary, non-productive stems. .
